# Audio Quality Rubric

This rubric describes how I evaluate voice, speech and spoken-word audio from both a technical and creative perspective.

---

## 1. Technical Cleanliness

I listen for technical issues that could distract the listener or reduce production quality.

Key criteria:

- noise floor
- clipping
- distortion
- mouth clicks
- plosives
- sibilance
- room reflections
- background noise
- edit artifacts
- inconsistent loudness
- unwanted processing artifacts

A technically clean voice should not call attention to the recording process. It should allow the listener to focus on the message, performance and emotion.

---

## 2. Intelligibility

A voice can sound beautiful but still fail if the listener has to work too hard to understand it.

I evaluate:

- word clarity
- consonant definition
- vowel shape
- pronunciation
- pacing
- phrasing
- language-specific articulation
- listener fatigue
- clarity at low playback volume
- clarity in context with music, ambience or other sound elements

For narration, dubbing, audiobooks and AI voice production, intelligibility is one of the most important quality factors.

---

## 3. Voice Character

I listen for the sonic identity of the voice.

Relevant qualities include:

- timbre
- warmth
- presence
- intimacy
- brightness
- depth
- texture
- perceived age
- emotional color
- consistency of character across the piece

A strong voice has a recognizable identity. In voice production, the goal is often to preserve that identity while improving clarity, consistency and context fit.

---

## 4. Performance

Voice quality is not only technical. Performance determines whether the result feels human and believable.

I listen for:

- rhythm
- phrasing
- pauses
- emphasis
- emotional credibility
- energy
- tension or relaxation
- breath structure
- natural variation
- connection to the content

A technically perfect voice can still feel flat. A great voice performance carries intention.

---

## 5. Context Fit

A voice must be evaluated in the context of the final use case.

I ask:

- Does the voice fit the project?
- Does it match the intended audience?
- Does it support the emotional direction?
- Does it work in the final medium?
- Does it remain consistent across the full piece?
- Does it sound natural next to other voices or production elements?
- Does it meet the client’s creative brief?
- Does it feel premium, intimate, energetic, cinematic or neutral where required?

The best voice is not always the most impressive voice in isolation. It is the voice that works best for the project.

---

## 6. Evaluation Table

| Area | What I listen for |
|---|---|
| Clarity | Is every word understandable without strain? |
| Timbre | Does the voice retain a coherent sonic identity? |
| Naturalness | Does it sound human, fluid and believable? |
| Articulation | Are consonants, vowels and transitions clean? |
| Emotion | Does the performance match the intended context? |
| Pacing | Are rhythm, pauses and phrasing convincing? |
| Noise | Are there artifacts, distortion, clicks or background issues? |
| Consistency | Does the voice remain stable across the full piece? |
| Context Fit | Does the voice work in the actual scene, product or delivery format? |

---

## 7. Practical Rating Logic

When reviewing voice material, I usually think in practical categories:

### Excellent

- clean and natural
- emotionally credible
- technically stable
- easy to understand
- consistent across the full piece
- ready for client delivery or further production

### Usable With Minor Fixes

- generally strong
- small clicks, noise or pacing issues
- minor tonal inconsistency
- can be fixed without damaging the performance

### Needs Revision

- distracting noise or artifacts
- unclear pronunciation
- unstable performance
- inconsistent tone or loudness
- does not match the brief

### Not Suitable

- heavy distortion or clipping
- excessive background noise
- poor room sound
- unnatural or unusable performance
- insufficient clean material
- source quality would limit the final result

My goal is always to identify not only what is wrong, but what decision would actually improve the final voice experience.
