# Client Workflow

This document outlines how I approach client-facing audio work, especially when creative feedback needs to be translated into concrete production decisions.

---

## 1. Understand the Brief

I start by clarifying the purpose of the project.

Key questions:

- What is the final use case?
- Who is the target audience?
- What should the voice or production feel like?
- Are there references?
- What should be avoided?
- What is the required delivery format?
- What is the timeline?
- How many review rounds are expected?
- Are there technical specifications?
- Are there language, pronunciation or brand-specific requirements?

A clear brief prevents unnecessary revisions and helps turn subjective expectations into practical decisions.

---

## 2. Translate Creative Language

Clients and creative teams often describe sound emotionally rather than technically.

Examples:

- “warmer”
- “more natural”
- “less robotic”
- “more premium”
- “closer”
- “more emotional”
- “less harsh”
- “more energetic”
- “more cinematic”
- “more like the reference”

My role is to translate this language into concrete audio decisions.

Examples:

| Client Feedback | Possible Production Translation |
|---|---|
| Warmer | Softer tonal balance, less harshness, more body |
| More natural | Less processing, better pacing, more organic phrasing |
| Less robotic | More variation, better pauses, more emotional movement |
| More premium | Cleaner editing, tighter consistency, polished tone |
| Closer | More intimacy, controlled ambience, stronger presence |
| Less harsh | De-essing, tonal smoothing, less upper-mid aggression |
| More energetic | Tighter pacing, stronger emphasis, more forward delivery |

This translation layer is one of the most important parts of professional audio work.

---

## 3. Establish a Test Section

For larger projects, I prefer starting with a short test section.

This allows the client and production team to align on:

- voice character
- tone
- pacing
- emotional direction
- technical quality
- loudness
- pronunciation
- revision expectations

A good test section saves time because the creative direction becomes clear before the full production is completed.

---

## 4. Iterate Efficiently

My preferred workflow is:

1. Receive brief and source material.
2. Clarify unclear points.
3. Prepare a short test or first pass.
4. Collect feedback.
5. Translate feedback into technical changes.
6. Compare alternatives where helpful.
7. Apply approved direction to the full project.
8. Perform final quality control.
9. Deliver clean files in the required format.

Short feedback loops are usually more effective than waiting until the end of a project to discover that the direction was wrong.

---

## 5. Communication Style

I keep communication clear, practical and client-friendly.

That means:

- no unnecessary technical overload
- clear explanation of options
- honest assessment of source material
- transparent revision logic
- structured file handling
- realistic delivery expectations
- calm communication under time pressure

The client should always understand what decision is being made and why it improves the final result.

---

## 6. Delivery Standards

Before delivery, I check:

- file naming
- format requirements
- loudness consistency
- unwanted noise
- clicks or edit artifacts
- pacing
- pronunciation
- emotional consistency
- version control
- client notes
- playback in context

I want the delivered files to be easy to review, easy to use and technically reliable.

---

## 7. Working Principle

Great audio work is not only about technical execution.

It is about listening carefully, understanding what the client actually means and turning that into a result that feels right.

This is especially important in voice and AI audio work, where small differences in pacing, tone, clarity or emotional nuance can completely change how human and believable a voice feels.
